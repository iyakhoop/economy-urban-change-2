Recap & Overview

Late Middle Age 1300 - 1500 European cities in decline; Low population growth; Little progress in economic development
Preindustrial Age 1500 - 1700 Economic and urban growth of the Atlantic traders; Colonialism, Slavery; Reformation, Scientific revolution
Early Industrial Revolution 1750 - 1850 Originated in Britain; Textile machinery; Steam engine, Coal, Iron; Increased agricultural productivity
Prime Industrial Revolution 1850 - 1900 Expansion to Europe, North America, Japan; Coal, Steel, Railways, Gas Lamps, Telegraph; Modern Capitalist Economy; Massive urbanization, Sanitation crisis
Late Industrial Revolution 1900 - 1950 Petroleum, Automobiles; Electricity, Telephone, Radio; New Business Management, Consumerism; Monopoly, Rising income gap, The Great Depression; WWI-II

Urbanization in Major Countries

Growth, Cities, and Immigration

Westward Expansion 1850 - 1900 Demand for agricultural products was increasing due to the growth of cities; Railroad expansion connecting to cities to ship crops, meat, tools, and materials
Gilded Age 1870 - 1900 An era of rapid economic growth in the Northern and Western US; An influx of millions of European immigrants; Bribery and corruption in politics; Social problems masked by a thin gold gilding
Progressive Era 1890 - 1920 Social activism and political reform; Addressing problems caused by industrialization, urbanization, immigration, and political corruption; Resisting the control of industrial capitalists; Prohibition of alcoholic beverages

The Roaring 20s (1920-30)

  • Economic prosperity reached its peak in major cities: London, New York, Chicago, Paris, Berlin
  • More people lived in cities than on farms.
  • Mass culture: Jazz, radio, movies (Hollywood!), automobiles, celebrities, sports, fashion, arts
  • The rise of media industries, chain stores, and mass-market advertising
  • Leisure time: better production technology and household machines
  • Personal credit: consumer society & materialism

The Roaring 20s (1920-30)

  • Women attain more rights and freedom: the right to vote; better jobs; contraception; household machines; liberated lifestyle
  • Conservative economics: pro-business; weakening labor union; lower personal income taxes and business profits; unregulated markets
  • Wider income gap and wealth accumulation
  • The Great Depression & Mass unemployment

Fordism and the industrial city

  • Changes in cities from the 1920s through mid-1970s
    • changes in the way people work
    • changes in the way industrial production is structured
    • changes in the organization of society as whole
  • Factory system developed in the early 1900s by Henry Ford in Detroit to mass-produce automobiles.

Fordism and the industrial city

  • Taylorism: carefully control of the production + The moving assembly line
  • Cut the cost of car production by one-half.
  • Workers are paid $5 a day, twice the average industrial wage at the time.
  • Ford Model T: reliable and simple
  • Result: an increase in both supply of, and demand for, the products

Keynesian and the New Deal: 1930-50

  • New theory to address the Great Depression
    • Governments should intervene to regulate the business cycle
    • Create more effective demand for goods and services using fiscal policy
  • US after World War II
    • Increasing government spending: The New Deal
    • Interstate and intra-urban highway expansion
    • Suburbanization

Neo-Fordism and Contemporary Cities

  • Mid-1970s: Economic growth started to stagnate
    • increasing costs of raw materials
    • insufficient investment in R&D
    • market saturation of mass-produced goods
    • repetitive and boring assembly line work
    • international competition of labor market
    • increasing safety and environmental regulations

Neo-Fordism and Contemporary Cities

  • New Economy and globalization
  • Information technology and global network
  • Flexible labor market: multiskilled workers
  • Subcontracting, Off-shoring and Decentralization
  • Results: Deindustrialization, New Industrial Clusters
